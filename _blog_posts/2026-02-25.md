As large language models are increasingly applied to tasks requiring long context, a fundamental theoretical question remains open: what is the minimum memory a model must maintain to capture long-range dependencies in natural language? While transformer architectures grow their key-value cache linearly with sequence length and state-space models (SSMs) use a fixed recurrent state, the theoretical justification for whether either approach is sufficient has been lacking. This work addresses that gap by analyzing the statistical structure of natural language itself, establishing a power-law scaling law for bipartite mutual information, and deriving a principled lower bound on the required growth rate of a model's internal memory.

---

### Why this is exciting

#### Physics → AI
- **Entanglement entropy as inspiration.** The framework is directly motivated by entanglement entropy scaling in quantum many-body physics, where area laws and sub-volume laws constrain tensor network representations and the classical simulatability of quantum states. The authors import this intuition into natural language, asking how much predictive information is shared between the past and future of a text sequence, and whether this quantity obeys a sub-volume law analogous to those found in physical systems.
- **A necessary condition derived from first principles.** Just as entanglement entropy bounds the bond dimension required for efficient tensor network representations of quantum states, the L²M condition bounds the history state dimension required for effective autoregressive language modeling. Both results reflect the same principle: representing a system with growing correlations requires a representation that grows accordingly.
- **Empirical confirmation of sub-volume law.** The sub-volume law growth of bipartite mutual information in natural language, long conjectured but never definitively established due to high-dimensional estimation challenges, is confirmed here using state-of-the-art LLMs as density estimators, providing clear power-law scaling across multiple datasets and model families.
- **Architecture evaluation before training.** The L²M condition provides a data-driven diagnostic: by measuring the bipartite mutual information scaling exponent of a given data domain, one can assess whether a proposed architecture's history state scaling is theoretically sufficient, prior to committing to expensive training runs.

#### AI → Physics
- **Information-theoretic constraints on neural quantum states.** The same framework may extend back to quantum many-body systems, offering an information-theoretic perspective on the minimal history state dimension required for neural network simulation of quantum states, complementing existing analyses grounded in entanglement entropy.
- **A unified treatment of diverse architectures.** The notion of the history state, defined as the smallest set of latent variables that fully characterizes a model's output conditional probability, provides a single information-theoretic framework for analyzing transformers (KV pairs), SSMs, and RNNs simultaneously, paralleling how bond dimension unifies the analysis of tensor network methods.

### What the paper does

(b) In autoregressive models, conditional distributions are parameterized through the history state $\mathbf{z}$, the latent variables that store past information; examples include recurrent states in SSMs or RNNs, and key-value pairs in transformers. (c) The maximum bipartite mutual information a model can express scales with $\mathrm{dim}(\mathbf{z})$; to model long contexts effectively, $\mathrm{dim}(\mathbf{z})$ must grow at least as fast as the power-law scaling of the true bipartite mutual information.*

#### 1) A bipartite mutual information scaling law in natural language
The bipartite mutual information $I^{BP}$ between two adjacent segments of a text sequence of length $L$ quantifies how much information the past carries about the future, and directly measures the amount of information from a preceding block that is predictive of the next block. Measuring this quantity has historically been difficult due to the curse of dimensionality faced by classical estimators such as K-nearest-neighbor methods and neural estimators like MINE and InfoNCE. The authors circumvent these limitations by using state-of-the-art LLMs as density estimators, enabling reliable computation of conditional log-likelihoods for long sequences. Using both a bias-corrected direct estimator and the vCLUB upper bound, they measure $I^{BP} \sim L^\beta$ across the PG19 and Wikipedia datasets, finding robust power-law scaling extending across thousands of tokens. The measured exponent $\beta$ is consistent across datasets, estimators, and model families, and is argued to be a systematic underestimate of the true scaling exponent.

The two quantities scale independently and capture fundamentally different aspects of long-range dependence in natural language.*

#### 2) Why bipartite, not two-point, mutual information
A key contribution of the paper is demonstrating that the more commonly studied two-point mutual information $$I^{TP}_d = I(X_i; X_j)$$ between pairs of individual tokens at distance $$d$$ is an incomplete and potentially misleading measure of the dependencies relevant to language modeling. The bipartite mutual information decomposes as $$I^{BP} = \sum_{i,j} I(X_i; Y_j \mid X_{1:i-1} Y_{1:j-1})$$, which is not equal in general to the naive aggregation of two-point mutual informations $$\sum_{i,j} I^{TP}_{j-i+\ell}$$. The paper constructs two families of multivariate Gaussian distributions with identical two-point mutual information scaling but dramatically different bipartite mutual information scaling (one power-law, one logarithmic), confirming that two-point mutual information alone cannot distinguish systems with fundamentally different long-range correlational structure.

#### 3) The L²M condition and validation across architectures
Combining the empirical scaling law with a theoretical capacity bound, the paper derives the Long-context Language Modeling (L²M) condition. Theorem 5.2 establishes that a model's expressible bipartite mutual information is bounded by $C \cdot \mathrm{dim}(\mathbf{z}) + \log M$, where $\mathbf{z}$ is the history state and $M$ is the vocabulary size. This bound is proved under three independent sets of mild assumptions: discrete (finite-precision) representations, the almost orthogonal directions property of neural network representations, and Lipschitz continuity of the history state mapping. The bound implies the L²M condition: for a model to be MI-capable across growing sequence lengths, $\mathrm{dim}(\mathbf{z}) \gtrsim L^\beta$. Transformers satisfy this naturally since their KV cache grows linearly; fixed-state SSMs and RNNs do not, and would require model size to scale with sequence length. The theory is validated on synthetic Gaussian data, where KL divergences are computable exactly, and on the PG19 dataset, where conditional NLL exhibits the predicted architecture-dependent behavior.

### A taste of the results

**The L²M condition.** For a model to be MI-capable across all sequence lengths, its history state must satisfy:

$$
\mathrm{dim}\!\left(\mathbf{z}^q_{L/2}\right) \;\gtrsim\; I^{BP}_{L/2;\,L} \;\sim\; L^{\beta}.
$$

Since the measured exponent satisfies $\beta < 1$, transformers with linearly growing KV caches satisfy this condition as single models, though with over-provisioning relative to the minimum required scaling. This gap between linear growth and the $L^\beta$ requirement suggests a concrete target for future architecture design: architectures that precisely match the required sublinear scaling could potentially achieve improved efficiency while maintaining the capacity to capture long-range dependencies. Fixed-state SSMs require model size to grow with sequence length to achieve the same MI-capability. Extrapolating from the measured scaling (which likely underestimates the true $\beta$), the bipartite mutual information for a sequence of one million tokens could exceed 60,000 nats, implying SSM recurrent state dimensions approaching one million would be required to maintain low KL divergence at such lengths.

### How it works (at a glance)

1. **Measure bipartite mutual information in natural language** Use state-of-the-art LLMs as density estimators to compute $I^{BP}$ via a bias-corrected direct estimator and the vCLUB upper bound, confirming power-law scaling $I^{BP} \sim L^\beta$ across datasets, model families, and bipartition ratios.
2. **Bound model capacity by history state dimension** Prove, under three independent sets of mild and practically motivated assumptions, that the bipartite mutual information expressible by any autoregressive model satisfies $I^{BP,q} \leq C \cdot \mathrm{dim}(\mathbf{z}) + \log M$.
3. **Derive and apply the L²M condition** Combine the empirical scaling law with the capacity bound to obtain the necessary condition $\mathrm{dim}(\mathbf{z}) \gtrsim L^\beta$, and apply it to classify common architectures as satisfying or violating the condition as single models or model series.
4. **Validate on synthetic and natural language data** Confirm the theory on synthetic multivariate Gaussian distributions (permitting exact KL divergence computation) and on the PG19 dataset, across GPT2, Mamba, and Mamba2 architectures at scales from 50M to 2.8B parameters.

### Limitations and outlook

- **Necessary but not sufficient.** The L²M condition addresses a model's capacity to capture long-range dependencies through the lens of bipartite mutual information and does not address other aspects of language modeling, such as reasoning capabilities or world knowledge. Understanding how this theoretical capacity translates to downstream task performance remains an important open question; the relationship likely depends on additional factors including optimization dynamics, architectural inductive biases, and task-specific requirements.
- **Estimation bias.** The LLM-based mutual information estimators likely underestimate the true scaling exponent $\beta$, since both the direct and vCLUB estimators tend to overestimate $I^{BP}$ at short sequence lengths and underestimate it at long sequence lengths, compressing the fitted exponent. Developing more accurate estimation methods for high-dimensional, long-sequence mutual information remains an important methodological challenge.
- **Next steps** Extending the framework to hybrid architectures that combine different mechanisms (such as local attention with fixed-size recurrence) represents a natural direction that could unify understanding of how diverse architectural choices affect long-context capabilities. On the architecture design side, the L²M condition points toward a concrete and previously missing target: since transformers over-provision their history state with linear growth while the data only requires $L^\beta$ scaling (with $\beta < 1$), there is a principled efficiency gap to close. Architectures that precisely match the required sublinear scaling could retain the long-context capability of transformers while approaching the computational efficiency of SSMs, and the bipartite mutual information scaling exponent $\beta$ measured from data provides a quantitative design criterion for tuning that growth rate. Applying the framework to other sequential domains, such as biological sequences (proteins, DNA) and computer code, is also particularly promising: different mutual information scaling behaviors in these domains could provide a principled explanation for observed differences in model requirements across domains, and could similarly guide domain-specific architecture choices. The framework may also offer a new information-theoretic lens on the minimal complexity of neural network quantum state simulators.
